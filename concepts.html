01Foundation models & pretraining

A foundation model is a large neural network trained once, on a huge amount of unlabeled data, in a way that it can later be adapted to many specific tasks. The training trick is self-supervised learning: instead of hand-labeled examples, the model is given a task it can grade itself on, for example "predict the hidden part of this input." For DNA, the data is reference genomes and the model learns the statistical regularities of sequence: motifs, codon structure, local "grammar."

The payoff is transfer: the general patterns learned during pretraining give you a strong starting point for narrow tasks (promoter detection, variant scoring) with far less labeled data than training from scratch.

In one line: pretrain once on raw genomes, reuse everywhere.

02Tokenization: turning ACGT into tokens

A model can't read raw letters; it reads tokens (integers). How you chop DNA into tokens is a real design choice, trading off sequence length against vocabulary size. Three common schemes:

Single-nucleotide. One token per base. Maximum resolution, but the longest sequences. Used by HyenaDNA and Evo.
k-mer. Fixed windows of k bases. DNABERT-1 used overlapping 6-mers; Nucleotide Transformer uses non-overlapping 6-mers. Overlapping k-mers leak information between adjacent tokens and are now generally avoided.
Byte-pair encoding (BPE). Learns frequent sub-sequences as tokens, compressing length. Used by DNABERT-2 and Mistral-DNA.

How BPE works. Start with just the four bases as the vocabulary. Scan the genome for the most frequent adjacent pair of tokens, merge it into a single new token, and repeat until you reach a target vocabulary size. Frequent substrings (common motifs and repeats) collapse into one token each, while rare stretches stay as smaller pieces. The result is a fixed-size vocabulary that compresses sequences (fewer tokens to process) and never hits an "unknown" base, since it can always fall back to single nucleotides. It's the same algorithm that tokenizes English in GPT-style models. That is why Mistral-DNA can reuse the Mistral architecture on DNA.

# BPE on a repetitive stretch: the vocabulary grows, the token count shrinks
start        A T G A T G A T G    # 9 tokens (vocab = A C G T)
+ merge TG   A TG A TG A TG       # 6 tokens (added "TG")
+ merge ATG  ATG ATG ATG          # 3 tokens (added "ATG")
In the tutorial: when you call AutoTokenizer.from_pretrained(...), this is the step turning your DNA string into the integers the model sees. Mistral-DNA (Part 1) uses BPE; Nucleotide Transformer (Part 2) uses fixed non-overlapping 6-mers. Same step, different chunking.

03Embeddings

An embedding is the model's internal numerical summary of a sequence, a vector of a few hundred or thousand numbers that places "similar" sequences near each other in space. You can pull embeddings out of a frozen model and feed them to a simple classifier, cluster them, or visualize them. They're the cheapest way to put a foundation model to work.

In the tutorial: Part 1 computes an embedding for a DNA sequence straight from Mistral-DNA, with no training at all.

04Encoders and decoders

Two architecture families dominate, and they differ in what they are trained to predict. That choice decides what each one is good for.

Encoders (BERT-style). These are trained by masked language modelling. A fraction of the tokens (often around 15%) is hidden, and the model predicts each missing token using the context on both sides at once. Because every position can see the whole sequence, the resulting per-token vectors are rich and context-aware. That makes encoders the natural choice for embeddings and for classification, such as labelling a sequence as promoter or not, or tagging each base as a splice site. They are not generative, so they cannot produce new sequence on their own. Examples include Nucleotide Transformer, DNABERT and GENA-LM. These are exactly the kind of model you fine-tune in Part 2 of the tutorial.

Decoders (GPT-style). These are trained by next-token prediction. The model reads left to right and predicts the next base from everything before it, so its attention is causal and only looks backward. This gives it a genuine probability for any sequence, which unlocks two things. It can generate new sequence one base at a time, and it can score an existing sequence by likelihood. That likelihood score is what powers zero-shot variant-effect prediction, where you compare a reference sequence against a mutated one. Examples include Mistral-DNA and Evo.

A related third family is not a "language model" at all. Supervised sequence-to-function models such as Enformer, Borzoi and AlphaGenome are trained directly on experimental measurements like expression or chromatin accessibility. Different objective, different use.

05Inside a transformer: attention heads & layers

A transformer turns a sequence of token embeddings into a richer, context-aware representation by stacking the same building block many times. The schematic shows one block; a real model stacks many of them.

Self-attention. Each token produces three vectors: a query, a key, and a value. A token's query is compared against every other token's key to decide how much attention to pay to it, and the token's new representation becomes a weighted blend of all the value vectors. This is how each position pulls in context from the rest of the sequence.

Attention heads. Rather than do this once, a transformer runs several attention operations in parallel. These are the heads. Each head has its own query, key and value projections, so different heads can specialise. One might track immediate neighbours, another a recurring motif, another a long-range partner. Their outputs are concatenated and mixed. This is multi-head attention, and the number of heads (often 12) is one of the model's core size knobs.

Feed-forward, residuals, normalisation. After attention, each token passes through a small feed-forward network. Residual connections, which add a block's input back to its output, and layer normalisation keep training stable as blocks stack.

Depth & parameters. Stacking many such blocks (the model's layers) lets representations grow progressively more abstract. The "parameter count" people quote (117M, 500M, 40B) is mostly the weights in the attention and feed-forward matrices across all those layers. For DNA the inputs are nucleotide or k-mer tokens rather than words, but the machinery is identical. That is exactly why the NLP toolkit transfers.

In genomics: interpretability work reads individual heads and features to see what a model attends to, for instance heads that align to exon–intron boundaries.

Best visual explainer: Jay Alammar, The Illustrated Transformer.

06Attention & the long-context problem

That all-to-all attention is powerful, but its cost grows with the square of sequence length. Biology is stubbornly long-range: an enhancer can regulate a gene 100,000 bases away. So genomics has pushed architectures that scale better than plain attention:

implicit long convolutions (Hyena → HyenaDNA; StripedHyena 2 → Evo 2), state-space models (Mamba → Caduceus), and dilated convolutions (Enformer). The context window (how many bases a model can see at once) is a first-class design decision here, not a footnote.

07Transfer learning & fine-tuning

Once you have a pretrained model, there are three ways to use it, in increasing order of cost:

Probing. Freeze the model, extract embeddings, train only a small classifier on top. Fast, cheap, a strong baseline.
Fine-tuning. Continue training the model's own weights on your labeled task. More accurate, more expensive, and prone to overfitting on small datasets.
Parameter-efficient fine-tuning. A middle path (next section).

08PEFT & LoRA

Full fine-tuning updates every weight in the model. It is accurate but memory-hungry. Parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) freezes the original model and trains only a small set of new parameters bolted on top.

LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation) is the most common form. Instead of updating a big weight matrix W directly, it learns a small low-rank correction ΔW = B·A, where A and B are tiny (rank r much smaller than the matrix width). You train A and B, leave W frozen, and add them back at inference. This cuts trainable parameters by orders of magnitude. That is exactly what makes fine-tuning a 500M-parameter model feasible on a free GPU.

Why low rank works. The change a pretrained model needs in order to specialise to one task turns out to live in a tiny subspace. A low-rank ΔW therefore captures almost all of it. That's why you can skip the vast majority of the parameters and lose very little accuracy.

# the saving, for one 1024 x 1024 weight matrix at rank r = 8
full fine-tune   1024 x 1024          = 1,048,576 params
LoRA (rank 8)    8 x (1024 + 1024)    =    16,384 params   # ~64x fewer

In the PEFT library it's a few lines, and the same config drives Part 2 of the tutorial:

# wrap a frozen base model with trainable LoRA adapters
from peft import LoraConfig, get_peft_model, TaskType
cfg = LoraConfig(
    task_type=TaskType.SEQ_CLS,          # classification head (promoter / enhancer)
    r=8, lora_alpha=16,                  # rank, and how strongly the update is scaled
    target_modules=["query", "value"],   # which layers get adapters (names are model-specific)
    lora_dropout=0.05,
)
model = get_peft_model(base_model, cfg)
model.print_trainable_parameters()       # trainable: <1% of all params
In the tutorial: Part 2 uses LoRA on NT-500M. Only the adapters are trained, so it finishes in minutes on Kaggle.

09Quantization (& QLoRA)

Model weights are normally stored as 32- or 16-bit floating-point numbers. Quantization stores them with fewer bits, typically 8-bit or 4-bit integers. The point is memory: a 7-billion-parameter model needs roughly 14 GB in 16-bit but only about 3.5 GB in 4-bit. That is often the difference between "won't load" and "fits on a free GPU." It can also speed up inference. The cost is a small, usually acceptable loss of precision; modern schemes (8-bit LLM.int8(), 4-bit NF4) are designed to keep that loss tiny.

How the bits are spent. A floating-point number divides its bits into a sign, an exponent (which sets the range of values) and a fraction/mantissa (which sets the precision). Lower-precision formats simply use fewer bits:

FormatBitsExponentFractionMemory / value
FP32328234 bytes
FP16165102 bytes
BFLOAT1616872 bytes
INT88–7 *1 byte
INT4 / NF44–– *≈ 0.5 byte

* Integer formats have no exponent: INT8 is 1 sign bit + 7 value bits. 4-bit NF4 (used by QLoRA) doesn't use a plain sign/exponent/fraction split (it places values on a nonlinear scale) but still needs only about half a byte per weight.

The classic worked example is storing π. Fewer fraction bits means the value gets rounded off sooner:

π = 3.14159265358979…   (true value)
FP32 · 4 bytes  →  3.1415927    # ~7 significant digits kept
FP16 · 2 bytes  →  3.140625     # ~3 significant digits kept

Halving the bits halves the memory per weight; 4-bit (NF4, used in QLoRA) halves it again to about 0.5 byte each. Across billions of weights that is what decides whether a model loads on a free GPU. For weights, the lost precision is usually tolerable.

In practice you turn it on when loading a model:

# load a model in 4-bit to fit a small GPU (needs bitsandbytes)
from transformers import AutoModel, BitsAndBytesConfig
cfg = BitsAndBytesConfig(load_in_4bit=True)
mdl = AutoModel.from_pretrained(name, quantization_config=cfg, trust_remote_code=True)

QLoRA combines the two ideas: keep the base model frozen in 4-bit, and train LoRA adapters on top of it. Because the frozen weights barely use memory, you can fine-tune models far larger than would otherwise fit on a single free GPU. It is the technique that put fine-tuning large models within reach of students and small labs.

Why it matters here: quantization is how you'd stretch a Kaggle GPU to load or fine-tune the bigger genomic models (e.g. larger Nucleotide Transformer checkpoints) rather than only the toy ones.

10Zero-shot variant scoring

One of the most useful tricks needs no labels at all. A decoder model assigns a likelihood to any sequence: how "expected" it looks given everything the model learned. To judge a mutation, you score the reference sequence and the mutated sequence and compare: a variant that sharply lowers the likelihood is one the model finds "surprising," which often flags it as potentially disruptive.

In the tutorial: Part 1 does exactly this for a CFTR cystic-fibrosis mutation, using log-likelihood from the DNA model. No training, no labels.

11Evaluation metrics

How do you know a fine-tuned model is any good? It depends on the data:

Accuracy. Fraction correct. Misleading when classes are imbalanced.
F1. Balances precision and recall; better for skewed data.
AUROC. How well the model ranks positives above negatives across all thresholds.
MCC (Matthews correlation coefficient). The most honest single number under heavy imbalance, because it accounts for all four cells of the confusion matrix at once.

In the tutorial: promoter prediction is scored with F1; the more imbalanced enhancer task uses MCC.

12Is DNA really a "language"? A reality check

The analogy that powers all of this (treat DNA like text and reuse the NLP toolkit) is productive but loose, and worth holding loosely. DNA has no clean word boundaries; its most important "rules" are biochemical and act over enormous distances; and a high model likelihood is not the same thing as a real biological function.

It is also genuinely contested how much genomic LMs add. A prominent strand of expert skepticism, from Anshul Kundaje (Stanford) among others, argues that much of the reported progress rests on flawed benchmarks; "bad benchmarks propagate," in his phrase. The recurring problems are concrete: data leakage between training and test sets; variant-effect benchmarks that ignore linkage disequilibrium and so overstate how well a model pinpoints the truly causal variant; and models tuned to win on surrogate objectives rather than real biological endpoints, optimised to pass the test rather than do the biology. On many functional-genomics tasks, well-designed supervised models (and sometimes simple baselines or plain sequence conservation) match or beat self-supervised gLMs, and the clean scaling wins seen in NLP have not clearly materialised. Recent probing work even finds gLMs leaning on surface base-composition rather than positional regulatory grammar, with scale amplifying the gap rather than closing it.

Crucially, this isn't anti-deep-learning: the same critics champion strong supervised models (SpliceAI, Enformer/Borzoi). The point is to treat gLMs as one tool among many: compare against strong supervised baselines and conservation, evaluate on rigorous benchmarks and real endpoints, and always validate on held-out data. Used that way they are powerful statistical priors over sequence, not oracles.

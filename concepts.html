01Foundation models & pretraining

A foundation model is a large neural network trained once, on a huge amount of unlabeled data, in a way that it can later be adapted to many specific tasks. The training trick is self-supervised learning: instead of hand-labeled examples, the model is given a task it can grade itself on — e.g. "predict the hidden part of this input." For DNA, the data is reference genomes and the model learns the statistical regularities of sequence: motifs, codon structure, local "grammar."

The payoff is transfer: the general patterns learned during pretraining give you a strong starting point for narrow tasks (promoter detection, variant scoring) with far less labeled data than training from scratch.

In one line: pretrain once on raw genomes, reuse everywhere.

02Tokenization — turning ACGT into tokens

A model can't read raw letters; it reads tokens (integers). How you chop DNA into tokens is a real design choice, trading off sequence length against vocabulary size. Three common schemes:

Single-nucleotide — one token per base. Maximum resolution, but the longest sequences. Used by HyenaDNA and Evo.
k-mer — fixed windows of k bases. DNABERT-1 used overlapping 6-mers; Nucleotide Transformer uses non-overlapping 6-mers. Overlapping k-mers leak information between adjacent tokens and are now generally avoided.
Byte-pair encoding (BPE) — learns frequent sub-sequences as tokens, compressing length. Used by DNABERT-2 and Mistral-DNA.

How BPE works. Start with just the four bases as the vocabulary. Scan the genome for the most frequent adjacent pair of tokens, merge it into a single new token, and repeat until you reach a target vocabulary size. Frequent substrings — common motifs and repeats — collapse into one token each, while rare stretches stay as smaller pieces. The result is a fixed-size vocabulary that compresses sequences (fewer tokens to process) and never hits an "unknown" base, since it can always fall back to single nucleotides. It's the same algorithm that tokenizes English in GPT-style models — which is why Mistral-DNA can reuse the Mistral architecture on DNA.

# BPE on a repetitive stretch — the vocabulary grows, the token count shrinks
start        A T G A T G A T G    # 9 tokens (vocab = A C G T)
+ merge TG   A TG A TG A TG       # 6 tokens (added "TG")
+ merge ATG  ATG ATG ATG          # 3 tokens (added "ATG")
In the tutorial: when you call AutoTokenizer.from_pretrained(...), this is the step turning your DNA string into the integers the model sees. Mistral-DNA (Part 1) uses BPE; Nucleotide Transformer (Part 2) uses fixed non-overlapping 6-mers — same step, different chunking.

03Embeddings

An embedding is the model's internal numerical summary of a sequence — a vector of a few hundred or thousand numbers that places "similar" sequences near each other in space. You can pull embeddings out of a frozen model and feed them to a simple classifier, cluster them, or visualize them. They're the cheapest way to put a foundation model to work.

In the tutorial: Part 1 computes an embedding for a DNA sequence straight from Mistral-DNA, with no training at all.

04Encoders vs decoders

Two architecture families dominate, distinguished by what they're trained to predict:

Encoders (BERT-style). Trained by masked language modeling — hide some tokens, predict them from both sides. Bidirectional context makes them excellent for embeddings and classification. → Nucleotide Transformer, DNABERT, GENA-LM.

Decoders (GPT-style). Trained by next-token prediction — predict the following base from everything before it. This enables generation and likelihood scoring. → Mistral-DNA, Evo.

A related third family isn't a "language model" at all: supervised sequence-to-function models (Enformer, Borzoi, AlphaGenome) trained directly on experimental measurements like expression or accessibility. Different objective, different use.

05Inside a transformer: attention heads & layers

A transformer turns a sequence of token embeddings into a richer, context-aware representation by stacking the same building block many times. Inside one block:

Self-attention. Each token produces three vectors — a query, a key, and a value. A token's query is compared against every other token's key to decide "how much should I pay attention to you?", and the token's new representation becomes a weighted blend of all the value vectors. This is how each position pulls in context from the rest of the sequence.

Attention heads. Rather than do this once, a transformer runs several attention operations in parallel — the heads. Each head has its own query/key/value projections, so different heads can specialise: one might track immediate neighbours, another a recurring motif, another a long-range partner. Their outputs are concatenated and mixed. This is multi-head attention, and the number of heads (e.g. 12) is one of the model's core size knobs.

Feed-forward, residuals, normalisation. After attention, each token passes through a small feed-forward network. Residual connections (adding the block's input back to its output) and layer normalisation keep training stable as blocks stack.

Depth & parameters. Stacking many such blocks — the model's layers — lets representations grow progressively more abstract. The "parameter count" people quote (117M, 500M, 40B) is mostly the weights in the attention and feed-forward matrices across all those layers. For DNA the inputs are nucleotide or k-mer tokens rather than words, but the machinery is identical — which is exactly why the NLP toolkit transfers.

In genomics: interpretability work reads individual heads and features to see what a model attends to — for instance, heads that align to exon–intron boundaries.

Best visual explainer: Jay Alammar, The Illustrated Transformer.

06Attention & the long-context problem

That all-to-all attention is powerful, but its cost grows with the square of sequence length. Biology is stubbornly long-range: an enhancer can regulate a gene 100,000 bases away. So genomics has pushed architectures that scale better than plain attention:

implicit long convolutions (Hyena → HyenaDNA; StripedHyena 2 → Evo 2), state-space models (Mamba → Caduceus), and dilated convolutions (Enformer). The context window — how many bases a model can see at once — is a first-class design decision here, not a footnote.

07Transfer learning & fine-tuning

Once you have a pretrained model, there are three ways to use it, in increasing order of cost:

Probing — freeze the model, extract embeddings, train only a small classifier on top. Fast, cheap, a strong baseline.
Fine-tuning — continue training the model's own weights on your labeled task. More accurate, more expensive, and prone to overfitting on small datasets.
Parameter-efficient fine-tuning — a middle path (next section).

08PEFT & LoRA

Full fine-tuning updates every weight in the model — accurate but memory-hungry. Parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) freezes the original model and trains only a small set of new parameters bolted on top.

LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation) is the most common form. Instead of updating a big weight matrix W directly, it learns a small low-rank correction ΔW = B·A, where A and B are tiny (rank r much smaller than the matrix width). You train A and B, leave W frozen, and add them back at inference. This cuts trainable parameters by orders of magnitude — which is exactly what makes fine-tuning a 500M-parameter model feasible on a free GPU.

In the tutorial: Part 2 uses LoRA on NT-500M — only the adapters are trained, so it finishes in minutes on Kaggle.

09Quantization (& QLoRA)

Model weights are normally stored as 32- or 16-bit floating-point numbers. Quantization stores them with fewer bits — typically 8-bit or 4-bit integers. The point is memory: a 7-billion-parameter model needs roughly 14 GB in 16-bit but only about 3.5 GB in 4-bit — often the difference between "won't load" and "fits on a free GPU." It can also speed up inference. The cost is a small, usually acceptable loss of precision; modern schemes (8-bit LLM.int8(), 4-bit NF4) are designed to keep that loss tiny.

How the bits are spent. A floating-point number divides its bits into a sign, an exponent (which sets the range of values) and a fraction/mantissa (which sets the precision). Lower-precision formats simply use fewer bits:

FormatBitsExponentFractionMemory / value
FP32328234 bytes
FP16165102 bytes
BFLOAT1616872 bytes
INT88–7 *1 byte
INT4 / NF44–– *≈ 0.5 byte

* Integer formats have no exponent: INT8 is 1 sign bit + 7 value bits. 4-bit NF4 (used by QLoRA) doesn't use a plain sign/exponent/fraction split — it places values on a nonlinear scale — but still needs only about half a byte per weight.

The classic worked example is storing π. Fewer fraction bits means the value gets rounded off sooner:

π = 3.14159265358979…   (true value)
FP32 · 4 bytes  →  3.1415927    # ~7 significant digits kept
FP16 · 2 bytes  →  3.140625     # ~3 significant digits kept

Halving the bits halves the memory per weight; 4-bit (NF4, used in QLoRA) halves it again to about 0.5 byte each. Across billions of weights that is what decides whether a model loads on a free GPU — and for weights the lost precision is usually tolerable.

In practice you turn it on when loading a model:

# load a model in 4-bit to fit a small GPU (needs bitsandbytes)
from transformers import AutoModel, BitsAndBytesConfig
cfg = BitsAndBytesConfig(load_in_4bit=True)
mdl = AutoModel.from_pretrained(name, quantization_config=cfg, trust_remote_code=True)

QLoRA combines the two ideas: keep the base model frozen in 4-bit, and train LoRA adapters on top of it. Because the frozen weights barely use memory, you can fine-tune models far larger than would otherwise fit on a single free GPU — the technique that put fine-tuning large models within reach of students and small labs.

Why it matters here: quantization is how you'd stretch a Kaggle GPU to load or fine-tune the bigger genomic models (e.g. larger Nucleotide Transformer checkpoints) rather than only the toy ones.

10Zero-shot variant scoring

One of the most useful tricks needs no labels at all. A decoder model assigns a likelihood to any sequence — how "expected" it looks given everything the model learned. To judge a mutation, you score the reference sequence and the mutated sequence and compare: a variant that sharply lowers the likelihood is one the model finds "surprising," which often flags it as potentially disruptive.

In the tutorial: Part 1 does exactly this for a CFTR cystic-fibrosis mutation, using log-likelihood from the DNA model — no training, no labels.

11Evaluation metrics

How do you know a fine-tuned model is any good? It depends on the data:

Accuracy — fraction correct. Misleading when classes are imbalanced.
F1 — balances precision and recall; better for skewed data.
AUROC — how well the model ranks positives above negatives across all thresholds.
MCC (Matthews correlation coefficient) — the most honest single number under heavy imbalance, because it accounts for all four cells of the confusion matrix at once.

In the tutorial: promoter prediction is scored with F1; the more imbalanced enhancer task uses MCC.

12Is DNA really a "language"?

The analogy that powers all of this — treat DNA like text and reuse NLP — is productive but loose, and it's worth holding loosely. DNA has no clean word boundaries; its most important "rules" are biochemical and act over enormous distances; and a high model likelihood is not the same thing as a real biological function. There's active debate about how much genomic LMs truly add over well-tuned, task-specific models on some benchmarks, and about how much benchmark scores reflect genuine biological insight versus dataset artifacts.

None of that diminishes the tools — it just means treating them as powerful statistical priors over sequence, not oracles. For a careful, balanced overview, the survey below is the best starting point.
